# Morning Lower-Body Mobility and Speed Primer

This note is a research-backed design for a short morning lower-body routine that aims to improve active range of motion, tissue readiness, and the movement qualities that support speed and explosiveness.

This design is an inference from the cited evidence, not a direct copy of any one study protocol.
The evidence supports dynamic mobility and sport-relevant movement preparation more strongly than passive stretching alone.
It does not support the idea that a long static stretching routine by itself is the best way to get faster or more explosive.

## What The Research Suggests

- Dynamic stretching tends to improve or at least preserve near-term force, power, sprint, and jump performance better than static stretching alone.
- Long static stretching before explosive work can reduce performance, especially when it exceeds about `60 s` per muscle group and is not followed by dynamic activity.
- Mobility training appears more likely to help or maintain performance than harm it over time, but specificity matters.
- Plyometric training is one of the clearest levers for improving sprint and jump ability, so a small elastic component makes more sense here than a mobility-only routine.
- The evidence does not say a daily routine is superior to `2 to 3` weekly mobility sessions, but a short daily routine is a practical way to accumulate high-quality movement without much fatigue if it stays controlled.

## Design Principles

1. Use active mobility, not long passive holds, as the base.
2. Cover the full lower-body chain: feet, ankles, calves, tibialis, quads, hamstrings, glutes, adductors, abductors, hip flexors, and hip rotation.
3. Pair mobility with positional strength and control so the gained range is usable.
4. Finish with a small elastic primer so the session supports speed rather than only flexibility.
5. Keep the default version short enough that it can actually happen every morning.

## The Ideal Daily Version

Target duration: `10 to 12 minutes`

### 1. Foot and ankle activation

- `Toe yoga`: `10` reps each side
  - Lift big toe while keeping the other four down, then reverse.
- `Ankle knee-over-toe rocks`: `10` reps each side
  - Keep heel down and drive the knee forward over the toes.
- `Tibialis raises`: `15` reps
  - Lean against a wall and lift toes hard toward the shins.

Why:
This opens the ankle, wakes up the foot, and gives the shin and calf complex some active work rather than only passive length.

### 2. Soleus and calf readiness

- `Bent-knee calf raises`: `12` reps each side with a `1 s` pause at the top
- `Straight-knee calf raises`: `10` reps each side

Why:
The lower leg matters for stiffness, force transfer, and elastic rebound.
Using both bent-knee and straight-knee positions covers the soleus and gastrocnemius more completely.

### 3. Hamstring and posterior-chain sweep

- `Hamstring sweeps`: `8` reps each side
  - Heel on floor, toe up, hinge back, then stand tall.
- `Single-leg hinge reach`: `6` slow reps each side

Why:
This gives the hamstrings length under control, plus hip-hinge patterning for posterior-chain recruitment.

### 4. Hip flexor, quad, and glute block

- `Split-squat hip-flexor iso`: `20 s` each side
  - Squeeze the glute of the trailing leg hard.
- `Split-squat pulses`: `6` reps each side
- `Leg swings front-to-back`: `10` reps each side

Why:
Speed mechanics need hip extension behind the body and fast hip flexion in front.
This block opens the front of the hip while keeping the glutes involved.

### 5. Adductor and lateral hip block

- `Adductor rock-backs`: `8` reps each side
- `Cossack squat shifts`: `6` reps each side

Why:
This covers the inner thigh, groin, lateral hip, and frontal-plane control that basic sagittal stretching misses.

### 6. Hip rotation block

- `90/90 hip switches`: `8` total reps
- `90/90 forward fold`: `3` gentle breaths each side

Why:
Hip internal and external rotation matter for clean stride mechanics, pelvic control, and durable loading.

### 7. Glute and trunk control

- `Single-leg glute bridge march`: `6` reps each side
- `Standing wall march hold`: `2 x 10 s` each side

Why:
Mobility without stance control leaks force.
This block ties the hips into the trunk and rehearses the marching positions that show up in sprinting.

### 8. Elastic speed primer

- `Pogo hops`: `2 x 20` contacts
- `A-march`: `2 x 10` steps each side

Why:
This is the piece that keeps the routine aimed at explosiveness instead of just feeling loose.
It is intentionally small so it can be repeated often without turning the morning into a workout.

## Hard-Day Version

Use this before a speed session, gym day, or race-day warm-up extension.

- Keep the full routine.
- Add `2 x 10 m` ankling.
- Add `2 x 10 m` A-skip.
- Add `2 x 3` broad-jump or bound contacts at submaximal effort.

This is the better place for a little more elastic work.
It should still feel sharp, not fatiguing.

## Recovery-Day Version

Use this if the legs feel flat, sore, or beaten up.

- Keep sections `1` through `7`.
- Cut pogo hops to `1 x 10` contacts or skip them entirely.
- Replace the elastic finisher with `2 minutes` of easy walking.

The goal on these days is quality movement, not forcing bounce.

## Muscle Group Coverage

| Area | Main exercises |
| --- | --- |
| Feet | toe yoga |
| Tibialis / shins | tibialis raises |
| Calves / soleus | bent-knee and straight-knee calf raises, pogos |
| Quads | split-squat pulses, Cossack shifts |
| Hamstrings | hamstring sweeps, single-leg hinge reach |
| Glutes | split-squat iso, bridge march, wall march |
| Hip flexors | split-squat hip-flexor iso, leg swings, wall march |
| Adductors / groin | adductor rock-backs, Cossack shifts |
| Abductors / lateral hip | Cossack shifts, single-leg stance work |
| Hip internal / external rotators | 90/90 switches and folds |

## Why This Is The Best Daily Compromise

- It is short enough to be sustainable.
- It prioritizes active ROM over passive flexibility.
- It uses sprint-relevant positions instead of generic stretching only.
- It includes enough positional strength and stiffness work to make the mobility usable.
- It includes a small plyometric dose, because explosiveness depends on force and elastic qualities, not just joint range.

## Guardrails

- Do not turn this into a long passive stretching session before speed work.
- Do not push the elastic block to fatigue.
- If a movement causes joint pain, sharp pain, or nerve-type symptoms, stop and replace it.
- If morning stiffness is very high, do one slower round first and add the pogo work only if the legs feel better, not worse.

## Bottom Line

If the aim is to get faster and more explosive, the best morning routine is not a pure mobility routine.
It is a lower-body movement primer built from:

- active mobility
- positional control
- sprint-relevant marching mechanics
- a tiny elastic dose

That combination is more defensible than trying to stretch every muscle passively every morning and hoping speed appears from extra range alone.
